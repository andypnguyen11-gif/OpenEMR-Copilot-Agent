The American Diabetes Association Standards of Care addresses
prevention or delay of type 2 diabetes in adults with prediabetes
(hemoglobin A1c 5.7 to 6.4 percent, fasting plasma glucose 100 to 125
mg/dL, or 2-hour plasma glucose 140 to 199 mg/dL on oral glucose
tolerance testing). First-line management is structured intensive
behavioral lifestyle change. Pharmacotherapy with metformin is an
adjunctive option in selected higher-risk patients.

The recommended lifestyle program targets a minimum 7 percent loss of
baseline body weight and at least 150 minutes per week of
moderate-intensity physical activity. Patients should be referred to
an intensive behavioral lifestyle intervention program modeled on the
Diabetes Prevention Program — for example, a CDC-recognized National
Diabetes Prevention Program. Dietary patterns associated with reduced
diabetes risk include Mediterranean, DASH, and plant-forward eating
patterns; the specific pattern can be individualized to patient
preferences. Reduced consumption of sugar-sweetened beverages and
processed foods is consistently emphasized.

Metformin therapy for diabetes prevention should be considered in
adults with prediabetes, particularly those with body mass index 35
kg/m² or greater, those younger than 60 years of age, and women with
prior gestational diabetes. The recommendation reflects evidence that
the relative risk reduction with metformin is greatest in these
groups, while the absolute benefit in older lower-risk adults is
modest. Long-term metformin use is associated with biochemical
vitamin B12 deficiency; periodic measurement of B12 is recommended,
particularly in patients with anemia or peripheral neuropathy.

Patients with prediabetes should have annual monitoring with
hemoglobin A1c or fasting plasma glucose. Cardiovascular risk
factors should be assessed and treated to standard targets;
prediabetes itself is associated with increased cardiovascular risk
independent of progression to diabetes. Statin therapy decisions
follow general atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease risk
assessment. Hypertension and dyslipidemia management proceed under
the relevant cardiovascular guidelines, not under diabetes-specific
glycemic targets, until type 2 diabetes is established.

Women with a history of gestational diabetes warrant lifelong
screening for diabetes every 1 to 3 years given approximately 50
percent lifetime risk of progression to type 2 diabetes. Lifestyle
intervention and metformin are both reasonable preventive options in
this group; metformin has supportive evidence specifically in
postpartum women with prior gestational diabetes.

Iron-deficiency anemia in adults is most commonly screened with a
complete blood count and ferritin. Serum ferritin reflects total body
iron stores and is the single most useful test for diagnosing iron
deficiency in the absence of inflammation. In adults without active
inflammatory illness, a serum ferritin below approximately 30 ng/mL
is consistent with iron deficiency; ferritin in the 30 to 100 ng/mL
range may still indicate deficiency in patients with chronic disease,
chronic kidney disease, or active inflammation, since ferritin is an
acute-phase reactant. A low ferritin with a low or low-normal
transferrin saturation supports the diagnosis.

Initial work-up of newly identified iron deficiency in adults should
include evaluation for menstrual blood loss in premenopausal women,
gastrointestinal blood loss in adults at risk, dietary intake, and
malabsorption (including celiac disease and prior gastric surgery).
Concurrent autoimmune thyroid disease can co-occur with iron
deficiency and is worth noting on the differential.

Oral iron is first-line for uncomplicated iron-deficiency anemia.
Ferrous sulfate 325 mg (65 mg elemental iron) once daily, or every
other day, is the typical adult regimen; alternate-day dosing
improves fractional absorption and is generally better tolerated.
Vitamin C (or an acidic juice) co-administered improves absorption.
Calcium-containing supplements, antacids, proton pump inhibitors,
levothyroxine, and tetracyclines should be separated from oral iron
by at least 2 hours. Reassess hemoglobin in 4 to 8 weeks; ferritin
and transferrin saturation typically lag and are rechecked at 8 to
12 weeks. Failure to respond to a trial of oral iron warrants
evaluation for ongoing blood loss, malabsorption, or an alternative
cause of anemia.

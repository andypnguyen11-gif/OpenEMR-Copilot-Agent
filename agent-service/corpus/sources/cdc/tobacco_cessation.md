title: Tobacco use cessation counseling and pharmacotherapy (CDC / USPSTF)

All adults should be asked about tobacco use, and adults who use
tobacco should be advised to stop and offered both behavioral
counseling and pharmacotherapy. The combined approach approximately
doubles the likelihood of quit success compared to brief advice
alone.

First-line pharmacotherapy options include nicotine replacement
therapy (patch, gum, lozenge, inhaler, or nasal spray), varenicline,
and bupropion SR. Combination NRT (a long-acting patch with a
short-acting form for breakthrough cravings) is more effective than
single-form NRT.

Pregnant patients should be offered behavioral counseling; routine
pharmacotherapy use during pregnancy is not recommended owing to
limited safety data, though clinicians may consider NRT case by case
when behavioral support alone is insufficient.

For adults with cardiovascular disease, tobacco cessation reduces
the risk of recurrent cardiovascular events more than any single
medication. Cessation counseling should be offered at every clinical
encounter.

The CDC-led National Diabetes Prevention Program (National DPP) is a
structured year-long lifestyle change program for adults with
prediabetes or at high risk for type 2 diabetes. Eligibility is
established by any one of: a hemoglobin A1c of 5.7 to 6.4 percent, a
fasting plasma glucose of 100 to 125 mg/dL, a 2-hour plasma glucose of
140 to 199 mg/dL on oral glucose tolerance testing, or a previously
positive screening risk score. A history of gestational diabetes also
qualifies a patient for enrollment without requiring a current abnormal
glucose value.

The two primary behavioral targets of the program are a 5 to 7 percent
reduction in body weight and at least 150 minutes per week of
moderate-intensity physical activity (for example, brisk walking).
The curriculum is delivered by a trained lifestyle coach over a
12-month period: weekly or biweekly sessions for the first 6 months
focused on dietary change, physical activity, and behavioral
self-management, followed by monthly maintenance sessions for the
remainder of the year. Programs may be in-person, distance learning,
online, or combination delivery, and all CDC-recognized programs
follow the same evidence-based curriculum.

Outcome data from the original Diabetes Prevention Program trial
(referenced by the CDC for program design) showed a 58 percent
reduction in incidence of type 2 diabetes among participants in the
lifestyle arm compared with placebo, with the relative risk reduction
exceeding 70 percent in adults aged 60 years or older. The
Medicare Diabetes Prevention Program (MDPP) covers participation as a
preventive benefit for eligible Medicare beneficiaries. Patients can
locate a CDC-recognized program through the program's online registry.

Pharmacotherapy is not part of the CDC National DPP curriculum itself.
Metformin for diabetes prevention is addressed in clinical guideline
sources (NIH/NIDDK and ADA Standards of Care); the National DPP focus
is exclusively on supervised lifestyle change with measurable weight
and activity targets. Clinicians may refer patients to a recognized
DPP independent of any pharmacotherapy decision.

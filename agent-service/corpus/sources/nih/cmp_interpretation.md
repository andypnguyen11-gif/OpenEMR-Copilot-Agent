The comprehensive metabolic panel reports sodium, potassium, chloride,
bicarbonate, blood urea nitrogen (BUN), creatinine, glucose, calcium,
total protein, albumin, total bilirubin, alkaline phosphatase, AST,
and ALT. The panel anchors evaluation of acid-base status, renal
function, hepatic function, and metabolic state.

Serum sodium below 135 mEq/L is hyponatremia. Mild hyponatremia (130
to 134) is often dilutional in the outpatient setting, related to
thiazides, SSRIs, or excess water intake; sodium below 125 with
neurologic symptoms is a medical emergency requiring careful
correction at no more than 8 to 10 mEq/L per 24 hours to avoid osmotic
demyelination. Hypernatremia (sodium above 145) most commonly
reflects free-water deficit and is corrected with hypotonic fluid at a
similarly cautious rate.

Serum potassium of 5.5 mEq/L or higher is hyperkalemia and warrants
action: confirm with a non-hemolyzed sample, obtain an ECG when above
6.0 or with any conduction abnormality, and review medications
including ACE inhibitors, ARBs, potassium-sparing diuretics, and
non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs. Persistent potassium above 6.0
mEq/L or any value with ECG changes is treated emergently with
calcium, intracellular shift agents (insulin with glucose,
beta-agonists), and removal therapy. Hypokalemia below 3.5 mEq/L is
often diuretic- or vomiting-related; replace and assess for ongoing
losses, particularly in patients on digoxin or with cardiac arrhythmia
risk.

Acute kidney injury is defined by a creatinine rise of 0.3 mg/dL or
greater within 48 hours, a 50 percent rise from baseline within 7
days, or oliguria. Estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR) below
60 mL/min/1.73 m² persisting for at least three months establishes
chronic kidney disease; an isolated low eGFR may reflect acute
illness, dehydration, recent contrast, or laboratory variability and
should be confirmed before staging. A BUN-to-creatinine ratio above
about 20 favors a pre-renal pattern (volume depletion, GI bleed,
high-protein intake) over intrinsic renal disease.

AST or ALT elevations are interpreted relative to upper limit of
normal: less than 5 times upper limit is mild, 5 to 10 times is
moderate, and greater than 10 times is severe. ALT above AST favors
non-alcoholic fatty liver disease and chronic hepatitis; AST above ALT
in a 2-to-1 or higher ratio favors alcohol-related liver disease.
Alkaline phosphatase rises with cholestatic processes and bone
turnover; pair with gamma-glutamyl transferase to localize to the
liver.

Glucose interpretation depends on fasting status: a fasting glucose of
126 mg/dL or higher on two occasions, or a random glucose of 200 mg/dL
with classic symptoms, indicates diabetes; fasting glucose of 100 to
125 indicates impaired fasting glucose.

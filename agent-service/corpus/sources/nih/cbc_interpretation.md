The complete blood count reports concentrations of hemoglobin and
hematocrit, total and differential leukocyte counts, platelet count,
red cell indices including mean corpuscular volume (MCV), and red cell
distribution width. Interpretation depends on the patient's baseline,
clinical context, and the magnitude of any deviation, but
laboratory-typical adult reference and action thresholds are useful
anchors.

Anemia is defined in adults as a hemoglobin below approximately 13
g/dL in men and below approximately 12 g/dL in non-pregnant women.
Hemoglobin below 10 g/dL is moderate anemia; below 8 g/dL is severe.
The MCV partitions anemia into microcytic (MCV below 80 fL — iron
deficiency, thalassemia trait, anemia of chronic disease in some
cases), normocytic (MCV 80 to 100 fL — acute blood loss, anemia of
chronic disease, early iron deficiency, hemolysis, marrow
infiltration), and macrocytic (MCV above 100 fL — vitamin B12 or
folate deficiency, hypothyroidism, alcohol-related, drug-induced
including methotrexate or zidovudine).

White blood cell count above approximately 11,000 per microliter
(11.0 × 10⁹/L) is leukocytosis; with a left shift toward neutrophils,
this most commonly reflects acute infection, stress response, or
corticosteroid effect. WBC above 30,000 per microliter, persistent
neutrophilia without an obvious driver, or basophilia, eosinophilia,
or circulating blast forms warrant hematology evaluation. Neutrophil
percentage above 70 to 75 percent of total WBC supports a bacterial or
acute inflammatory process; lymphocyte predominance is more typical of
viral illness.

Platelet count below approximately 150,000 per microliter is
thrombocytopenia. Counts above 50,000 are typically asymptomatic;
spontaneous bleeding risk rises below 20,000, and platelets below
10,000 are an indication for inpatient evaluation and possible
transfusion. Sudden drops to under half of baseline or to under
100,000 within days warrant immediate work-up for drug-induced
thrombocytopenia (heparin-induced thrombocytopenia is a notable
emergency), immune thrombocytopenia, sepsis, or microangiopathic
hemolytic anemia.

Pancytopenia (low hemoglobin, low WBC, and low platelets simultaneously)
always warrants prompt evaluation for marrow failure, infiltration, or
acute systemic illness.

Primary hypothyroidism is diagnosed biochemically when serum
thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH) is elevated and free thyroxine
(free T4) is low. Subclinical hypothyroidism describes an elevated
TSH with a normal free T4; the typical reflex is to confirm the
finding with a repeat TSH at 6 to 8 weeks before initiating long-term
therapy.

Common causes include autoimmune (Hashimoto's) thyroiditis, prior
radioiodine or thyroid surgery, and certain medications. Symptoms can
include fatigue, cold intolerance, dry skin, constipation, weight
change, menstrual irregularity, and depressed mood; symptom severity
correlates loosely with biochemical severity.

Levothyroxine is the standard replacement. A common starting dose for
otherwise healthy nonelderly adults is approximately 1.6 mcg per kg
of ideal body weight per day, typically 50 to 100 mcg orally once
daily, taken on an empty stomach 30 to 60 minutes before food, coffee,
calcium, iron, or proton pump inhibitors. Lower starting doses
(25 to 50 mcg) are used in older adults or in patients with coronary
artery disease.

After initiation or dose change, recheck TSH no sooner than 6 to 8
weeks. The treatment goal in primary hypothyroidism is a TSH within
the laboratory reference range; in most adults this corresponds to a
TSH between roughly 0.5 and 4.5 mIU/L. A persistently elevated TSH on
therapy commonly reflects under-replacement, malabsorption (e.g.,
celiac disease), drug-drug interactions, or inconsistent dosing
relative to meals.

Once the patient is on a stable dose with TSH in range, repeat TSH
every 6 to 12 months, or sooner with new symptoms, weight change of
more than ~10 percent, pregnancy, or initiation of an interacting
medication.

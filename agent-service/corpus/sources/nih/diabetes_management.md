For most non-pregnant adults with type 2 diabetes, a hemoglobin A1c
target of less than 7 percent is reasonable, with individualization
toward 7.5 to 8 percent in older adults, those with limited life
expectancy, advanced complications, or a history of severe
hypoglycemia. A1c should be checked at least twice yearly when on
stable therapy at goal, and quarterly when therapy has changed or
glycemic targets are not being met.

Metformin remains first-line oral pharmacotherapy in patients without
contraindications. Initial dosing is typically 500 mg once or twice
daily with meals, titrated over weeks to a maintenance dose of 1000 mg
twice daily. Metformin is contraindicated when estimated glomerular
filtration rate (eGFR) is below 30 mL/min/1.73 m². For patients with
eGFR between 30 and 45, metformin is not initiated, and existing
therapy is dose-reduced and reassessed. Hold metformin around
iodinated-contrast imaging and acute illness.

When A1c remains above goal on metformin, second-line therapy is
selected by comorbidity. In patients with established atherosclerotic
cardiovascular disease, heart failure, or chronic kidney disease, an
SGLT2 inhibitor (empagliflozin, dapagliflozin) or a GLP-1 receptor
agonist (semaglutide, liraglutide, dulaglutide) is preferred for
demonstrated cardiorenal benefit independent of glycemic effect. For
patients without these comorbidities, the choice is guided by weight,
hypoglycemia risk, and cost, with sulfonylureas, DPP-4 inhibitors,
basal insulin, and thiazolidinediones as additional options.

Glycemic monitoring beyond A1c includes self-monitoring of blood
glucose for patients on insulin or sulfonylureas. Continuous glucose
monitoring should be considered for patients on multiple daily insulin
injections or with frequent hypoglycemia. Annual screening for diabetic
nephropathy (urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio plus eGFR), retinopathy,
and neuropathy is part of routine diabetes management.

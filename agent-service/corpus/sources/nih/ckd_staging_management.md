Chronic kidney disease is staged by estimated glomerular filtration
rate (eGFR), expressed in mL/min/1.73 m². Stage G1 corresponds to an
eGFR of 90 or higher with evidence of kidney damage; G2 is 60 to 89
with evidence of damage; G3a is 45 to 59; G3b is 30 to 44; G4 is 15 to
29; and G5 is below 15 (kidney failure). Albuminuria adds a parallel
dimension: A1 is below 30 mg/g, A2 is 30 to 300, and A3 is above 300
mg of albumin per gram of creatinine on a spot urine albumin-to-
creatinine ratio.

A confirmed eGFR of 60 or below for at least three months establishes
chronic kidney disease independent of cause. Repeat the measurement to
confirm — a single low eGFR can reflect acute illness, dehydration,
recent contrast exposure, or laboratory variability. BUN-to-creatinine
ratio interpretation is supplementary; isolated BUN elevation often
reflects volume depletion, gastrointestinal bleeding, or high-protein
intake rather than intrinsic renal disease.

Most patients with CKD and either hypertension, diabetes, or
albuminuria above 30 mg/g should be on an ACE inhibitor (lisinopril) or
ARB (losartan). These agents reduce intraglomerular pressure and slow
CKD progression independent of their blood-pressure effect. SGLT2
inhibitors (dapagliflozin, empagliflozin) further reduce CKD
progression in patients with eGFR above 20 and albuminuria, and are now
recommended in CKD with or without diabetes.

Drug dose adjustments are required at lower eGFR. Metformin is
contraindicated below eGFR 30 and dose-reduced between 30 and 45.
Avoid NSAIDs in CKD when eGFR is below 60. Avoid gadolinium-based
contrast at eGFR below 30 unless essential. Reassess potassium,
creatinine, and bicarbonate at intervals appropriate to the stage —
every 3 months in stage G3b or G4, more frequently with active
intercurrent illness or after dose changes to renin-angiotensin
agents.

Refer to nephrology for eGFR below 30, rapid eGFR decline, A3
albuminuria, refractory hypertension or hyperkalemia, or
unexplained-cause CKD.

title: Metformin monitoring — renal function, B12, and lactic acidosis risk (NIH/NIDDK)

Patients on metformin should have renal function assessed at least
annually with serum creatinine and estimated glomerular filtration rate
(eGFR). Initial labs are obtained before starting therapy. Metformin is
contraindicated when eGFR is below 30 mL/min/1.73 m². For an eGFR of
30 to 45, metformin is not initiated; if a patient is already on
therapy and the eGFR drops into this range, dose-reduce by half and
reassess every three months. Increase monitoring frequency in patients
with progressive chronic kidney disease, advanced age, congestive heart
failure, hepatic impairment, or concomitant nephrotoxic medications.

Long-term metformin use is associated with reduced vitamin B12
absorption. Periodic measurement of serum B12 is reasonable in patients
on metformin for several years, particularly those with peripheral
neuropathy, anemia, or megaloblastic features on a complete blood
count. Mean corpuscular volume (MCV) above 100 fL on a CBC should
prompt B12 testing.

Hold metformin around iodinated-contrast imaging in patients with eGFR
between 30 and 60, in patients receiving intra-arterial contrast, or in
those with conditions associated with reduced renal perfusion (heart
failure, sepsis, dehydration). Resume after 48 hours when renal
function has been re-evaluated and is stable.

Lactic acidosis is rare but serious. Risk is elevated by acute kidney
injury, decompensated heart failure, sepsis, alcohol misuse, and
hepatic failure. Counsel patients to hold metformin and seek care for
significant gastrointestinal upset, malaise, or muscle pain, especially
in the context of dehydration or acute illness.

Adults with prediabetes — defined by a hemoglobin A1c of 5.7 to 6.4
percent, a fasting plasma glucose of 100 to 125 mg/dL, or a 2-hour
plasma glucose of 140 to 199 mg/dL on oral glucose tolerance testing —
are at substantially elevated risk of progression to type 2 diabetes.
Approximately one in three U.S. adults has prediabetes, and without
intervention a substantial proportion progress to type 2 diabetes
within five years. The cornerstone of management is structured
lifestyle change, with metformin considered as an adjunct in selected
higher-risk patients.

The lifestyle-change targets are a 5 to 7 percent loss of baseline
body weight and at least 150 minutes per week of moderate-intensity
physical activity. These targets are most reliably achieved within a
structured intensive behavioral counseling program such as a
CDC-recognized National Diabetes Prevention Program. Dietary
recommendations emphasize reduced caloric and saturated-fat intake
with increased fiber, with no single specific macronutrient pattern
required. Self-monitoring of weight and physical activity, regular
contact with a lifestyle coach, and goal-setting are central
behavioral components.

Metformin may be considered for type 2 diabetes prevention in adults
with prediabetes, with the strongest supporting evidence in three
groups: adults under 60 years of age, adults with body mass index of
35 kg/m² or greater, and women with a history of gestational
diabetes. Use of metformin for prediabetes is off-label in the United
States. Typical dosing is 850 mg once daily titrated to 850 mg twice
daily as tolerated, with patient counseling on gastrointestinal side
effects and the recommendation for periodic vitamin B12 monitoring on
long-term therapy. Metformin is not initiated when estimated
glomerular filtration rate is below 45 mL/min/1.73 m² and is
contraindicated below 30 mL/min/1.73 m².

Patients with prediabetes should be re-screened for diabetes annually
with hemoglobin A1c or fasting plasma glucose. Cardiovascular risk
factor screening — blood pressure, lipid panel, smoking status, and
weight — should be performed at the same visit, as the same lifestyle
interventions reduce cardiovascular risk independent of glycemic
progression. Patients who progress to a fasting plasma glucose of 126
mg/dL or higher on two occasions or an A1c of 6.5 percent or higher
have crossed the threshold to type 2 diabetes and require management
under that diagnosis.

Asthma management is staged by severity and control. The 2020 NAEPP
focused update from NHLBI organizes therapy into six steps; the goals
of treatment are to maintain normal activity, minimize symptoms,
preserve lung function, and avoid exacerbations and side effects.
Persistent moderate asthma is treated with a daily inhaled
corticosteroid in combination with a long-acting beta agonist
(ICS-LABA), commonly fluticasone-furoate / vilanterol or
fluticasone-propionate / salmeterol, with as-needed short-acting
bronchodilator (albuterol) for rescue. The single-maintenance-and-
reliever (SMART) regimen using budesonide-formoterol once or twice
daily and as needed is supported in steps 3 and 4 for patients age 4
years and older.

Spirometry is the recommended objective measure of asthma control. An
FEV1 that is 60 to 79 percent of predicted with bronchodilator
responsiveness suggests moderate persistent disease; FEV1 below 60
percent reflects severe persistent disease. Reassess at every step
change and at least annually. Frequent rescue albuterol use (more than
twice weekly other than for pre-exercise prophylaxis) or any oral
corticosteroid course in the past year suggests inadequate control and
warrants step-up.

A written asthma action plan is a core element of management for all
persistent asthma. The plan defines green-yellow-red zones by symptoms
or peak flow, lists controller and reliever doses, and specifies when
to escalate care.

Asthma in pregnancy: poorly controlled asthma poses greater risk to
the fetus than the medications used to control it. Inhaled
corticosteroids are first-line and have the most pregnancy safety
data; budesonide is the most-studied ICS in pregnancy, but other ICS
already maintaining control can be continued. Long-acting beta
agonists (salmeterol, formoterol) and as-needed albuterol are
considered safe in pregnancy. Continue leukotriene receptor
antagonists (montelukast) only if needed for control; biologics are
case-by-case. Pre-conception planning emphasizes optimizing control
before pregnancy, ensuring an updated asthma action plan, reviewing
inhaler technique, confirming influenza and Tdap vaccination, and
counseling on smoking and trigger avoidance.

Allergic rhinitis frequently coexists with asthma; treating rhinitis
with intranasal corticosteroids and second-generation antihistamines
(cetirizine, loratadine, fexofenadine) improves overall airway control
and is compatible with pregnancy.

Vitamin D deficiency (serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D below 20 ng/mL) is
common and is replaced with cholecalciferol (vitamin D3) 1000 to 2000
IU daily, with rechecking after 8 to 12 weeks. Pre-pregnancy and
pregnancy 25-OH vitamin D goals are typically 30 ng/mL or higher.

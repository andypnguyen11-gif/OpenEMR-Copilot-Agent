Insulin therapy is added in type 2 diabetes when non-insulin agents
fail to achieve the individualized A1c target despite adequate
adherence and dose escalation. Common triggers for adding basal
insulin are an A1c that remains above 9 percent on combination oral
therapy, a fasting plasma glucose persistently above 180 mg/dL, the
presence of catabolic features (weight loss, ketosis, classic
symptoms), or hyperglycemic emergencies. A GLP-1 receptor agonist
(semaglutide, dulaglutide, liraglutide) is generally preferred over
basal insulin as the first injectable in patients without symptomatic
hyperglycemia or acute illness because it preserves weight benefit and
is comparably effective at A1c reduction up to about 1.5 to 2 points.
When the A1c remains above target on combination metformin plus a
GLP-1 receptor agonist, basal insulin is the next step.

Starting basal-insulin doses are 10 units once daily or 0.1 to 0.2
units per kg of body weight once daily, given at bedtime or in the
morning. Long-acting analogs glargine U-100, glargine U-300, and
degludec U-100 or U-200 are preferred over NPH for lower nocturnal
hypoglycemia risk and once-daily convenience. Continue metformin and
the GLP-1 receptor agonist when starting basal insulin; reduce or stop
sulfonylureas if previously prescribed, given hypoglycemia overlap.

Titrate basal insulin to a fasting glucose target of 80 to 130 mg/dL
in most patients (relax to 100 to 150 in older adults or those at
hypoglycemia risk). A common protocol is to increase by 2 units every
3 days until the target is met; alternatively, increase by 1 unit
every day until target. Reduce dose for any hypoglycemic event below
70 mg/dL or recurrent symptoms. Reassess A1c at 3 months; if A1c
remains above target despite a fasting glucose at goal, post-prandial
hyperglycemia is the residual driver and prandial insulin or
intensification of the GLP-1 receptor agonist may be needed.

Insulin therapy in type 2 diabetes does not require routine
self-monitoring beyond fasting glucose for titration, but checks
before lunch, dinner, and bedtime are useful when intensifying or when
hypoglycemia is suspected. Counsel on hypoglycemia recognition,
glucagon availability for severe events, sick-day rules, and insulin
storage. Coexisting depression should be screened for and treated, as
untreated depression strongly predicts non-adherence and
hyperglycemia.

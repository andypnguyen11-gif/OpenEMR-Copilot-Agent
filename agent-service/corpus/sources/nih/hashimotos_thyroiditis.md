Hashimoto's disease is a chronic autoimmune lymphocytic thyroiditis
and is the most common cause of primary hypothyroidism in iodine-
sufficient regions. Antibodies against thyroid peroxidase (TPO) are
positive in roughly 90 percent of patients with Hashimoto's
hypothyroidism, and antibodies against thyroglobulin are positive in
a substantial minority. Either antibody can also be present in
euthyroid relatives without active disease.

A single TPO antibody titer is sufficient to support an autoimmune
etiology when an elevated TSH and a low or low-normal free T4 are
already documented. Repeat antibody testing has limited additional
clinical value once the autoimmune diagnosis is established; titers
do not reliably track disease activity or response to therapy and are
not used to titrate levothyroxine.

Patients with Hashimoto's disease are at increased risk for other
autoimmune conditions, including type 1 diabetes, Addison's disease,
celiac disease, vitiligo, and pernicious anemia, so a careful
review of systems and a low threshold for evaluating new symptoms are
appropriate. A small proportion of patients also have an associated
iron-deficiency state, partly reflecting impaired absorption and
partly the gastric autoimmunity that can accompany the syndrome.

Management of Hashimoto's hypothyroidism mirrors management of any
other primary hypothyroidism: levothyroxine titrated to a TSH within
the laboratory reference range, with periodic monitoring after the
patient is stable. There is no role for routine immunosuppressive
therapy.

Adults seen in primary care should be screened for unhealthy alcohol
use at least annually. AUDIT-C is the recommended brief screen: three
items totaling 0 to 12 points, with a positive screen at 4 or higher
in men and 3 or higher in women. A positive AUDIT-C is followed by the
full 10-item AUDIT or by direct clinical interview to characterize
severity. Standard-drink counts (one standard drink equals 14 g of
ethanol — roughly 12 oz of regular beer, 5 oz of wine, or 1.5 oz of
distilled spirits) anchor the conversation and the diagnostic
thresholds: low-risk drinking is up to 14 drinks per week and 4 per
day for men, and up to 7 per week and 3 per day for women;
heavy-drinking days exceed those daily limits.

Brief behavioral counseling for unhealthy alcohol use in primary care
reduces drinking and is a USPSTF Grade B recommendation for adults.
Components include feedback on the patient's pattern, comparison to
recommended limits, motivational interviewing, goal setting, and
arranged follow-up.

Alcohol use disorder is diagnosed by DSM-5 criteria; the count of met
criteria sets severity (mild 2 to 3, moderate 4 to 5, severe 6 or
more). FDA-approved medications for alcohol use disorder are
naltrexone (oral 50 mg daily or extended-release injection 380 mg
monthly), acamprosate (666 mg three times daily), and disulfiram (250
to 500 mg daily). Naltrexone is first-line for most adults including
those who continue drinking; it is contraindicated with current
opioid use or acute hepatitis. Acamprosate is preferred when liver
disease precludes naltrexone or when abstinence is the explicit goal.
Disulfiram requires committed abstinence and a supervised
administration arrangement.

Alcohol-related liver disease ranges from steatosis to hepatitis to
cirrhosis. Laboratory pattern in alcohol-related hepatitis classically
shows AST elevated more than ALT in a ratio of about 2 to 1, with
both typically below 300 to 500 U/L. Total bilirubin elevation, INR
prolongation, and a Maddrey discriminant function above 32 identify
patients with severe alcoholic hepatitis who may benefit from
specialty evaluation. Alkaline phosphatase rise is variable and may
reflect cholestatic injury. Order a right-upper-quadrant ultrasound
when biliary obstruction is in the differential, and pursue hepatology
referral for transaminases above 5 times upper limit, persistent
hyperbilirubinemia, or any feature of decompensation.

Counsel every patient with unhealthy alcohol use on the bidirectional
relationship between alcohol and depression, sleep, hypertension, and
medication interactions; offer warm hand-off to behavioral-health
support and follow up within 4 weeks.

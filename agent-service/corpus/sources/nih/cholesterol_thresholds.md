A standard fasting lipid panel reports total cholesterol, LDL
cholesterol, HDL cholesterol, and triglycerides. Reference ranges
commonly printed on U.S. lab reports include:

* Total cholesterol: less than 200 mg/dL (desirable).
* LDL cholesterol: less than 100 mg/dL (optimal); 130 mg/dL or higher
  is borderline-to-high and is one criterion for the dyslipidemia
  risk factor used in cardiovascular risk calculators.
* HDL cholesterol: greater than 40 mg/dL in men and greater than 50
  mg/dL in women is desirable; values below 40 mg/dL count as a
  cardiovascular risk factor.
* Triglycerides: less than 150 mg/dL (normal).

A non-HDL cholesterol value (total minus HDL) of 130 mg/dL or higher
roughly tracks an LDL of 100 mg/dL or higher and is increasingly
used as a treatment threshold in patients with elevated
triglycerides where calculated LDL is unreliable.

Initial management for borderline values is typically lifestyle
modification (diet, physical activity, weight management); statin
therapy is layered in based on overall cardiovascular risk per the
USPSTF / AHA-ACC primary-prevention guidance.

The U.S. Preventive Services Task Force concludes that the current
evidence is insufficient to assess the balance of benefits and harms of
screening for thyroid dysfunction in nonpregnant, asymptomatic adults.
This is a Grade I statement.

This Grade I statement applies only to screening of asymptomatic adults
who do not have clinical signs or symptoms of thyroid dysfunction and
are not pregnant. It does not apply to follow-up of patients with prior
thyroid disease, abnormal prior thyroid testing, or symptoms suggestive
of thyroid dysfunction (fatigue, unintentional weight change, cold
intolerance, abnormal menses, goiter), in whom evaluation with TSH is
clinically indicated.

When TSH testing is performed, an elevated serum thyroid-stimulating
hormone with a low or low-normal free thyroxine (free T4) is the
biochemical pattern of overt primary hypothyroidism. An isolated
elevated TSH with a normal free T4 is the pattern of subclinical
hypothyroidism. Confirmation of an abnormal TSH with a repeat
measurement, typically several weeks later, is recommended before a
lifelong treatment decision is made.

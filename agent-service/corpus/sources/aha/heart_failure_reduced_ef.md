Heart failure with reduced ejection fraction is defined by a left
ventricular ejection fraction of 40 percent or below with symptoms or
signs of heart failure. The 2022 ACC/AHA/HFSA heart-failure guideline
identifies four foundational drug classes — collectively guideline-
directed medical therapy, or GDMT — that are initiated and up-titrated
to target dose in every HFrEF patient who tolerates them. The four
classes are renin-angiotensin system inhibition (preferably an
angiotensin receptor-neprilysin inhibitor such as sacubitril-valsartan,
otherwise an ACE inhibitor or angiotensin receptor blocker), an
evidence-based beta blocker, a mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist,
and an SGLT2 inhibitor.

Renin-angiotensin therapy: an ARNI (sacubitril-valsartan) is preferred
over an ACE inhibitor or ARB when feasible and tolerable; switch from
ACEi to ARNI requires a 36-hour washout to avoid angioedema. In
patients with documented ACE-inhibitor angioedema, do not re-challenge
with another ACEi; an ARB (losartan, valsartan) can be substituted
with caution because cross-reactivity is small but non-zero. ARNI is
generally avoided after ACEi-angioedema as well.

Beta blockade in HFrEF is restricted to one of the three agents proven
to lower mortality: carvedilol, metoprolol succinate (extended
release), or bisoprolol. Metoprolol tartrate (immediate release) is
not interchangeable. Up-titrate every two weeks to the target dose as
heart rate and blood pressure permit, holding at the highest tolerated
dose if target is not reached.

Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (spironolactone, eplerenone)
are added in patients with NYHA class II to IV symptoms, ejection
fraction at or below 35 percent, and adequate renal function and
potassium. Initiation requires an eGFR above 30 mL/min/1.73 m² and a
serum potassium below 5.0 mEq/L. Recheck potassium and creatinine at
2 to 4 weeks after start or dose change, then every 3 to 6 months.

SGLT2 inhibitors (dapagliflozin, empagliflozin) reduce heart-failure
hospitalization and cardiovascular death across the ejection-fraction
spectrum, including HFrEF, with or without diabetes. They are
continued at eGFR as low as 20 to 25 mL/min/1.73 m² in heart failure;
hold around volume depletion or sustained hypotension.

Loop diuretics (furosemide, torsemide, bumetanide) are dosed for
volume control as needed and titrated to euvolemia; they do not modify
mortality and are not part of the four GDMT pillars. Monitor weight
trends and renal function during diuretic adjustments.

B-type natriuretic peptide (BNP) and N-terminal pro-BNP are useful for
diagnosis, prognosis, and monitoring. A BNP above 100 pg/mL or
NT-proBNP above 300 pg/mL supports heart failure as the cause of
dyspnea; markedly elevated values (BNP greater than 500 pg/mL,
NT-proBNP above 900 to 1800 pg/mL by age band) carry prognostic
weight. Trends matter more than single values once therapy is in
place.

Anemia in heart failure is common and most often reflects iron
deficiency or chronic kidney disease. Hemoglobin below the lower
adult reference (12 g/dL women, 13 g/dL men) warrants iron-status
labs (ferritin, transferrin saturation); IV iron is reasonable for
symptomatic iron-deficient HFrEF.

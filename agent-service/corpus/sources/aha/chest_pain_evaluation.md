---
title: Initial evaluation of acute chest pain in the outpatient setting (adapted from AHA/ACC 2021)
source: AHA
source_url: https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000001029
date: 2021-10-28
topics: [chest pain, ACS, troponin, ECG, cardiac]

Patients who present with new-onset chest pain that is suggestive of
an acute coronary syndrome should be evaluated promptly. Initial
evaluation includes a focused history and physical, a 12-lead ECG
within 10 minutes of presentation, and high-sensitivity cardiac
troponin measurement.

Pretest likelihood of obstructive coronary artery disease can be
estimated using validated risk scores (e.g. HEART score). Low-risk
patients with non-ischemic ECG changes and undetectable
high-sensitivity troponin can frequently be evaluated in the
outpatient setting; intermediate- and high-risk patients warrant
emergency department referral.

Concerning features that argue for emergency evaluation include
chest pain at rest, pain associated with diaphoresis or syncope,
ischemic ECG changes, troponin elevation, or known coronary artery
disease. Worsening exertional chest pain in a patient with
established cardiac disease (for example, prior myocardial
infarction or known multivessel CAD) should be treated as unstable
angina until proven otherwise.

Cardiac risk factors that raise pretest likelihood include older
age, male sex, family history of premature coronary disease,
diabetes, hypertension, dyslipidemia, and current tobacco use.

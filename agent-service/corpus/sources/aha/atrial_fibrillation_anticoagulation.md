Atrial fibrillation is the most common sustained arrhythmia in adults
and roughly quintuples stroke risk when untreated. The 2023 ACC/AHA/
ACCP/HRS atrial-fibrillation guideline recommends estimating stroke
risk with the CHA₂DS₂-VASc score and offering oral anticoagulation
for any score of 2 or higher in men and 3 or higher in women. A score
of 1 in men and 2 in women is a shared-decision indication;
patient-level bleeding risk and preferences determine whether
anticoagulation is started. CHA₂DS₂-VASc components are congestive
heart failure, hypertension, age 75 or older (2 points), diabetes,
stroke or TIA (2 points), vascular disease, age 65 to 74, and female
sex.

Direct oral anticoagulants (DOACs) — apixaban, rivaroxaban, edoxaban,
and dabigatran — are first-line for non-valvular atrial fibrillation
in most patients. Apixaban 5 mg twice daily is the typical dose, with
dose reduction to 2.5 mg twice daily when two of three criteria are
met: age 80 or older, weight 60 kg or less, or serum creatinine 1.5
mg/dL or higher. Warfarin is preferred for moderate-to-severe mitral
stenosis or mechanical heart valves; INR target is 2 to 3 for most
indications.

Bleeding-risk stratification with HAS-BLED supports clinician judgment
but does not by itself contraindicate anticoagulation: hypertension,
abnormal renal or hepatic function, prior stroke, prior bleeding,
labile INR, age above 65, antiplatelet/NSAID use, and alcohol
excess. A high HAS-BLED score should prompt modification of
addressable risk factors (uncontrolled blood pressure, NSAIDs,
excessive alcohol) rather than simple anticoagulant withdrawal.

A patient on a DOAC who develops new microcytic anemia or otherwise
unexplained hemoglobin drop warrants an evaluation for occult
gastrointestinal bleeding. Initial work-up includes an upper and lower
endoscopic evaluation (EGD plus colonoscopy) when no obvious bleeding
source is identified externally, especially when prior colorectal
screening is overdue. Hold the DOAC for procedures per
agent-specific timing (apixaban: hold 24 to 48 hours pre-procedure for
high-bleeding-risk endoscopy depending on renal function), resume
post-procedure once hemostasis is confirmed and the bleeding source
is identified or excluded.

Combined antiplatelet plus anticoagulant therapy (for example, aspirin
plus apixaban) carries a higher bleeding risk and is reserved for a
specific indication (recent acute coronary syndrome, recent stenting)
with the shortest acceptable duration. Long-term aspirin is not
indicated solely as stroke prophylaxis when oral anticoagulation is in
place.

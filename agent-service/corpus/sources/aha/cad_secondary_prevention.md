title: Coronary artery disease — secondary prevention pharmacotherapy after MI (AHA)

After acute coronary syndrome, the foundation of secondary prevention
is dual antiplatelet therapy, a beta blocker, a high-intensity statin,
and — when indicated — an ACE inhibitor or angiotensin receptor
blocker.

Dual antiplatelet therapy combines aspirin 81 mg daily with a P2Y12
inhibitor (clopidogrel, ticagrelor, or prasugrel). The standard
duration after acute coronary syndrome or coronary stenting is 12
months, with shorter regimens (1 to 6 months) considered in patients
at high bleeding risk and longer courses considered in patients at
high ischemic risk and acceptable bleeding risk. After the dual
antiplatelet course completes, aspirin 81 mg daily continues
indefinitely.

Beta blockade with a cardioselective agent (metoprolol succinate,
bisoprolol, carvedilol) is initiated early after myocardial infarction
and continued for at least 12 months. In patients with reduced left
ventricular ejection fraction, beta blockade continues indefinitely.
Choose metoprolol succinate (extended-release) over metoprolol tartrate
when long-term once-daily dosing and ejection-fraction protection are
the goals.

High-intensity statin therapy (atorvastatin 40 to 80 mg or rosuvastatin
20 to 40 mg) is started in-hospital or at discharge and continued
indefinitely, targeting an LDL reduction of 50 percent or greater and
an LDL below 70 mg/dL. Add ezetimibe and consider PCSK9 inhibitor when
LDL remains above goal.

ACE inhibitors (lisinopril) or ARBs are indicated for patients with
left ventricular dysfunction, heart failure, hypertension, diabetes, or
chronic kidney disease, and reasonable for all patients with stable
coronary artery disease. Aldosterone-receptor antagonists are added in
post-MI patients with ejection fraction below 40 percent and either
heart failure symptoms or diabetes, provided potassium and renal
function permit.

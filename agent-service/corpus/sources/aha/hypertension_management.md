The 2017 American College of Cardiology / American Heart Association
hypertension guideline defines stage 1 hypertension as a systolic blood
pressure of 130 to 139 mm Hg or a diastolic of 80 to 89 mm Hg, and
stage 2 as 140/90 or higher. Diagnosis is established with at least two
elevated office readings on separate occasions, ideally confirmed with
home or ambulatory blood pressure monitoring.

For most adults with hypertension, the recommended treatment target is
a blood pressure below 130/80 mm Hg. This target applies to patients
with established cardiovascular disease, type 2 diabetes, chronic
kidney disease, and a 10-year atherosclerotic cardiovascular disease
risk of 10 percent or higher. Stage 1 hypertension in lower-risk adults
is initially managed with lifestyle therapy (DASH-style diet, sodium
reduction, weight loss, alcohol limitation, aerobic activity); stage 2
or high-risk stage 1 starts pharmacotherapy concurrently with
lifestyle.

First-line antihypertensive classes are thiazide-type diuretics
(chlorthalidone, hydrochlorothiazide), calcium channel blockers
(amlodipine), ACE inhibitors (lisinopril, enalapril), and angiotensin
II receptor blockers (losartan, valsartan). In Black adults without
heart failure or chronic kidney disease, initial therapy is typically a
thiazide or calcium channel blocker. In patients with chronic kidney
disease or proteinuria, ACE inhibitors or ARBs are preferred. Beta
blockers are not first-line monotherapy unless there is a compelling
indication such as ischemic heart disease, heart failure with reduced
ejection fraction, or atrial arrhythmia.

When monotherapy is insufficient, combine agents from different classes
rather than maximizing one. ACE inhibitors and ARBs should not be used
together. Reassess blood pressure within 4 weeks of starting or
adjusting therapy; once at goal, follow-up every 3 to 6 months is
typical.

title: ACE inhibitor and ARB monitoring — potassium, creatinine, and adverse effects (AHA)

After starting or up-titrating an ACE inhibitor (lisinopril, enalapril,
ramipril) or angiotensin receptor blocker (losartan, valsartan,
candesartan), recheck serum potassium and creatinine within 1 to 2
weeks. Reasonable laboratory tolerances are a serum creatinine rise of
up to about 30 percent above baseline and a serum potassium up to 5.5
mEq/L; values within these bounds typically reflect expected
hemodynamic effects on the renal efferent arteriole rather than
intrinsic injury.

A creatinine increase greater than 30 percent above baseline, or a
potassium of 5.5 mEq/L or higher, warrants holding the agent and
investigating volume status, concurrent NSAID or potassium-sparing
diuretic use, dietary potassium load, and the possibility of bilateral
renal artery stenosis. After resolution, restart at a lower dose with
closer monitoring; do not co-prescribe an ACE inhibitor with an ARB,
and avoid combining either with direct renin inhibitors.

A persistent dry cough develops in roughly 5 to 20 percent of patients
on an ACE inhibitor and is a class effect mediated by bradykinin
accumulation. The cough resolves after discontinuation, typically
within 1 to 4 weeks; transition to an ARB if the indication for
renin-angiotensin blockade remains.

Angioedema is a rare but life-threatening adverse effect. It can occur
months or years after therapy initiation and is more common in Black
patients. Suspect angioedema with any acute facial, lip, tongue, or
glottic swelling: discontinue the agent permanently, avoid all
angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors, and use ARBs only with
caution given a small cross-reactivity rate.

Once stable on therapy, recheck potassium and creatinine annually, or
sooner with intercurrent illness, dehydration, dose changes, or the
addition of an interacting agent.

The 2018 ACC/AHA cholesterol guideline groups patients into four
statin-benefit categories. Patients with clinical atherosclerotic
cardiovascular disease (ASCVD) — prior myocardial infarction, ischemic
stroke, transient ischemic attack of atherosclerotic origin, peripheral
arterial disease, or coronary or other arterial revascularization —
are candidates for high-intensity statin therapy regardless of baseline
LDL cholesterol. High-intensity statins lower LDL by approximately 50
percent or more and include atorvastatin 40 or 80 mg daily and
rosuvastatin 20 or 40 mg daily.

For primary prevention, an LDL cholesterol of 190 mg/dL or higher is
itself an indication for high-intensity statin therapy without
formal risk calculation. Adults aged 40 to 75 with diabetes are
candidates for at least moderate-intensity statin therapy regardless of
estimated risk; high-intensity statin is reasonable when ASCVD risk is
elevated or the patient has multiple cardiovascular risk factors.

For other adults aged 40 to 75 without diabetes and with LDL between
70 and 189 mg/dL, the 10-year ASCVD risk score (Pooled Cohort
Equations) drives the decision: borderline risk (5 to 7.5 percent)
favors lifestyle plus consideration of moderate-intensity statin if
risk-enhancing factors are present (family history of premature ASCVD,
chronic kidney disease, metabolic syndrome, persistent inflammatory
disease, or non-traditional lipid markers); intermediate risk (7.5 to
20 percent) favors moderate-intensity statin therapy; high risk (20
percent or higher) favors high-intensity statin.

LDL response is reassessed 4 to 12 weeks after initiation and after
dose changes, then every 3 to 12 months at steady state. In patients on
high-intensity statin therapy who do not achieve at least a 50 percent
LDL reduction or whose LDL remains above 70 mg/dL with very high
ASCVD risk, ezetimibe is added; PCSK9 inhibitors may be considered
when LDL remains above goal on maximally-tolerated statin plus
ezetimibe.
